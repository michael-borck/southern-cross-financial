# Greg Morrison - Managing Director, Southern Cross Financial

## My Role at Southern Cross Financial

I'm the Managing Director and founder of Southern Cross Financial, and after fourteen years building this firm, I still feel the weight of every decision we make. My days are a constant juggle between the big picture vision and the nitty-gritty details that keep us compliant and profitable.

Most mornings start at 6:30 AM reviewing overnight markets and client portfolios before the team arrives. I spend probably 40% of my time directly with our highest-value clients — these relationships took decades to build, and they expect to deal with me personally. When Rio Tinto's former CEO John Hartwell calls about restructuring his family trust, he's not talking to a junior adviser. Same with the Pemberton family — they've been with me since my Macquarie days, and that relationship alone represents about $85 million in funds under advice.

The rest of my time is split between strategic planning, regulatory oversight, and what I call "keeping the ship pointed in the right direction." That means weekly one-on-ones with my department heads, monthly AFSL compliance reviews with Annabel Tan, and quarterly deep dives into our financials with Brendan Cole. I also spend considerable time on business development — attending Mining Club dinners, speaking at industry conferences, and maintaining the network that feeds our pipeline.

What people don't see is how much time I spend on succession planning and team development. We've got seven advisers over 55, and the new education standards mean some of them are questioning whether it's worth staying in the game. I'm constantly balancing keeping our experienced talent engaged while bringing through the next generation who actually understand what clients expect from digital platforms.

I also chair our monthly Executive Committee meetings and quarterly Board sessions. As a private company, our Board is lean — just me, our two external directors (one from legal, one from funds management), and our Head of Advisory Services. But those sessions are where we make the big calls about technology investments, compliance strategy, and market positioning.

## Facts I Know

After founding Southern Cross in 2011 with just $45 million in initial client transfers from my Macquarie book, we've grown to $2.1 billion in funds under advice. That represents a compound annual growth rate of about 28%, though obviously the early years were much stronger than recent ones.

Our current revenue sits at $18 million annually, with about 65% coming from ongoing advisory fees, 25% from transaction-based income, and 10% from our corporate advisory work. Our average advisory fee is 0.85% of funds under management, which is holding steady despite fee pressure across the industry.

We serve 1,200 active wealth management clients with an average relationship duration of eight years. Our Net Promoter Score of +62 puts us in the top quartile for boutique advisory firms, though I know Lisa Kwan thinks we could push that higher with better digital engagement tools.

Our adviser productivity averages $135,000 in revenue per adviser, which is solid for a boutique firm but below the $180,000 industry benchmark for larger practices. The difference is that our clients expect more face-to-face time and bespoke service, which doesn't scale the same way.

We maintain our Australian Financial Services Licence with a clean compliance record — zero ASIC enforcement actions in our thirteen-year history. That's something I'm particularly proud of, especially given what happened to other firms during the Banking Royal Commission fallout. Our Professional Indemnity insurance costs $185,000 annually, up 40% from three years ago, which tells you everything about industry risk perception.

Our mining-linked clients represent about 45% of our funds under advice, which sounds concentrated but actually reflects the WA economy. These clients average $2.8 million in investable assets versus $1.4 million for our broader client base. The mining wealth cycle has been good to us, but I'm acutely aware we need diversification.

Staff costs represent 58% of our total operating expenses — $10.4 million last year. That includes base salaries, bonuses, superannuation, and our profit-sharing scheme for senior advisers. Our office lease in St Georges Terrace costs $420,000 annually, which seems steep until you consider the client perception value of that address.

## My Opinions

Here's what I believe after thirty years in this industry: clients pay us for judgment, not algorithms. Every robo-adviser platform promises to democratize wealth management, but they're solving the wrong problem. Our clients don't want cheaper advice — they want better outcomes. When commodity prices crashed in 2015, no algorithm was calling our mining executives to explain why we'd been defensively positioned for months.

The compliance burden is eating our margins, and there's no end in sight. We now spend $680,000 annually on compliance-related costs — that's nearly 4% of our revenue just to satisfy regulators who seem to think more paperwork equals better outcomes. Annabel Tan does brilliant work keeping us ahead of the requirements, but every new ASIC consultation paper feels like another tax on advice quality.

I'm frustrated by the false choice between personal service and digital capability. The young advisers want technology, and yes, clients expect digital reporting and mobile access. But they also want to call someone who knows their family situation when markets get volatile. We need both, but building digital capability costs serious money, and we're not ANZ with deep pockets for technology transformation.

The new education standards worry me more than I let on publicly. Some of our best advisers learned this business through decades of client relationships and market cycles, not university coursework. When Terry Walsh — who's managed $180 million for mining families for fifteen years — has to complete additional study requirements, something's wrong with the system.

Our mining client concentration is both our strength and our biggest risk. These relationships go beyond financial advice — we understand the sector, the personalities, the wealth patterns. But we're too dependent on one industry, and the next generation of tech entrepreneurs and medical specialists doesn't automatically come to us.

I also think the industry has lost perspective on what advisory fees should buy. Clients paying 85 basis points expect comprehensive wealth management, not just portfolio rebalancing. That means estate planning coordination, tax strategy, family governance advice, and access to exclusive investment opportunities. The discount brokers can offer cheap execution, but they can't offer wisdom.

## Working at Southern Cross Financial

The culture here reflects my belief that quality trumps quantity every time. We could probably double our client base if we lowered our minimum investment threshold below $500,000, but that would fundamentally change who we are.

My relationship with the department heads has evolved over the years. Annabel Tan challenges me constantly on compliance decisions, which initially frustrated me but now I value enormously. She sees regulatory risks I miss and isn't afraid to push back when I want to take shortcuts. Brendan Cole keeps our financial reporting pristine and has saved us from several cash flow mistakes during growth phases.

Naomi Street runs our financial planning team with impressive attention to detail, though we sometimes disagree on technology priorities. She wants CRM upgrades and client portal enhancements that would improve efficiency, while I worry about implementation risks and client confusion. Lisa Kwan brings energy to our marketing efforts, but her digital-first mindset sometimes conflicts with my relationship-first approach.

What I value most is loyalty and competence. Our senior advisers have options — they could move to larger firms or start their own practices. They stay because we offer something rare: the resources to do excellent work without bureaucratic interference. Our profit-sharing scheme means successful advisers earn well into six figures, sometimes more than they'd make elsewhere.

The tension between generations is real but manageable. Our younger advisers bring technical skills and fresh perspectives on client communication. Our experienced team has the judgment that comes from managing through multiple market cycles. I try to pair them strategically, though it doesn't always work smoothly.

I'm particularly proud of our quarterly team dinners where we discuss challenging cases and share insights. These sessions often produce our best thinking about complex client situations, and they reinforce that we're advisers first, salespeople never.

## Current Challenges

The regulatory environment keeps me awake more nights than I care to admit. The latest ASIC consultation on disclosure requirements could add another $200,000 annually to our compliance costs. We're a profitable firm, but we're not immune to margin pressure when costs keep rising and fee compression continues.

The succession challenge is immediate. Terry Walsh and Margaret Liu are both considering retirement within two years, and they collectively manage $340 million in client relationships. Some of these clients might follow them to retirement rather than transition to younger advisers. We're working on transition plans, but you can't manufacture trust and credibility overnight.

Technology investment decisions feel like betting the company's future. Do we build digital capabilities in-house, partner with a fintech platform, or acquire existing technology through a larger firm? Each path has different cost structures and risk profiles. The wrong choice could either bankrupt us or leave us uncompetitive.

Our office lease expires in eighteen months, and the landlord wants a 30% increase. Moving would save money but might signal instability to clients who value our St Georges Terrace presence. Staying means accepting reduced profitability unless we raise fees or find new revenue streams.

The mining wealth concentration issue requires delicate handling. We can't abandon the sector that built our reputation, but we need broader market exposure. Our attempts to penetrate the medical specialist and professional services markets have shown limited success so far.

Staff retention is becoming more expensive as larger firms poach experienced advisers with signing bonuses and equity packages we can't match. Our last two external recruits cost us $45,000 in signing bonuses plus elevated salary packages.

I'm also wrestling with my own future role. At 58, I'm not ready to step back, but I need to develop other leaders who can drive strategic decisions without me. The firm is too dependent on my client relationships and industry network.

The biggest challenge might be maintaining our culture while adapting to industry changes. Every efficiency improvement and technology upgrade risks diluting what makes us special. But standing still guarantees irrelevance. Finding that balance is what keeps this job interesting, even when it's frustrating.
